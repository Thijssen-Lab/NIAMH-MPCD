Additional theoretical background#

Transport coefficients#

As any other transport coefficient, the viscocity estimates of an MPCD fluid, both dynamical (\(\eta\)) and kinematic \(\nu = \eta/\rho = \eta/(\langle N_c\rangle m/a^d\)) viscocities, have kinematic and collisional contributions.

Consider the case of a diluted gas. In this case, the kinematic viscocity is dominated by its kinematic contribution, namely, the momentum carried by fluid particles to other regions of space. This can be estimated to be approximately the thermal velocity \(\sqrt{k_\text{B} T/m}\) times the mean free path \(\lambda = \delta t \sqrt{k_\text{B} T/m}\) [Gompper2009].

(1)#\[\nu_{\text{kin}} \sim \delta t \frac{k_\text{B} T}{m}.\]

Since mass \(m\) and thermal energy \(k_\text{B}T\) are unity to set the natural simulation units, the kinetic viscosity is simply increases linearly with time step \(\delta t\). This scaling ignores the specific role of density \(\left\langle N_c \right\rangle\), dimensionality \(d\) or any algorithm specific parameters (such as angle \(\theta\) in SRD or drag coefficient \(\gamma\) in Langevin MPCD). These differ for each MPCD algorithm but can be captured through an auxiliary function \(f_\text{kin}(\langle N_c\rangle,d)\) as

\[\begin{eqnarray} \nu_{\text{kin}} =& \delta t \frac{k_B T}{m}\, f_\text{kin}(\langle N_c\rangle,d). \end{eqnarray}\]

As density increases, the fluid is no longer a dilute gas and so momentum stops being primarely carried out by moving particles. Rather is transported through collision events. As such, the collision contribution to the viscocity becomes dominant. Since collisions occur once every \(\delta t\) in cells of size \(a\), simple scaling suggests that collisions contribute to the viscocity approximately as

(2)#\[\nu_{\text{col}}\sim \frac{a^2}{\delta t}.\]

As such, when the mean free path is small compared to the cell size, the collisional contribution dominates. As in the kinetic contribution, an auxiliary function \(f_\text{col}(\theta,\langle N_c\rangle,d)\) describes the number density, dimensionality and algorithm specific effects, such that

\[\begin{eqnarray} \nu_{\text{col}} =& \frac{a^2}{\delta t} f_\text{col}(\theta,\langle N_c\rangle,d). \end{eqnarray}\]

In general, the total kinematic viscocity is the sum of kinematic and collisional terms.

\[\nu = \nu_{\text{kin}} + \nu_{\text{col}}.\]

Contribution
